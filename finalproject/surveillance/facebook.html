Cambridge Analytica Scandal

In 2018, it was revealed that the political consulting firm Cambridge Analytica had improperly accessed the personal data of up to 87 million Facebook users. This data was then used to create targeted political advertising campaigns during the 2016 U.S. presidential election. The scandal raised concerns about the privacy practices of social media platforms and the potential misuse of personal data for political purposes.

Facebook has agreed to pay $725 million to resolve a class-action lawsuit that was accused of allowing third parties, including Cambridge Analytica, to access users' personal information. Cambridge Analytica worked for Donald Trump's successful presidential campaign in 2016, and gained access to the personal information from millions of Facebook accounts for the purposes of voter profiling and targeting.

Cambridge Analytica obtained that information without users' consent from a researcher who had been allowed by Facebook to deploy an app on its social media network that harvested data from millions of its users.

The Cambridge Analytica scandal fueled government investigations into its privacy practices. In 2019, Facebook agreed to pay $5 billion to resolve a Federal Trade Commission’s investigation into its privacy practices and $100 million to settle U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission claims that it misled investors about the misuse of users' data.

Cambridge Analytica's methods and their high-profile clients, including the Trump presidential campaign and the UK's Leave.EU campaign, brought the problems of psychological targeting that scholars have been warning against to public awareness. The scandal sparked an increased public interest in privacy and social media's influence on politics. The online movement #DeleteFacebook trended on Twitter.

This is an example of surveillance capitalism in action. Facebook allowed the collection huge amounts of user data without consent, analyzed and predicted future behavior, then sold this information to advertisers, political groups and corporations, turning human experince into a profitable product.
